Experimenting an Elementary Single-Carrier M-QAM Digital Communication Chain

Project Scope: This work explores the end-to-end processing of a QAM-based digital communication system. We analyze the impact of frequency-selective multipath channels, additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN), and receiver oscillator phase noise on signal integrity and spectral characteristics.

1. System Model & Parameters

The system utilizes a baseband equivalent approach, focusing on transmit pulse shaping (RRC), linear channel distortions, and receiver filtering.

Parameter Value Description
Symbol Rate (R) 10 MHz Rate of information transfer
Oversampling (r) 4 Samples per symbol pulse
Modulation 16-QAM / M-QAM Quadrature Amplitude Modulation order
Pulse Filter Root-Raised Cosine Roll-off factor Î± = 0.20

2. Transmitter Processing

Transmitter processing involves mapping random bits to QAM symbols followed by upsampling and RRC filtering to constrain the signal bandwidth.

% Pulse Shaping Implementation
alpha = 0.20; 
N_symbols_per_pulse = 40;
gt = rcosdesign(alpha, N_symbols_per_pulse, r, 'sqrt');
xt = filter(gt, 1, symbols_upsampled);

Task 1 Analysis

Observing the TX spectrum confirms the bandwidth occupancy of approximately $(1+\alpha)R$. The use of RRC filtering minimizes Inter-Symbol Interference (ISI) while ensuring spectral efficiency.

3. Channel Impairments

We modeled three distinct channel profiles (b1, b2, b3) ranging from an ideal line-of-sight to a harsh frequency-selective multipath environment.

Fading and Noise Scaling

A critical component of this project is understanding the scaling of noise relative to the oversampling factor. The noise scaling factor accounts for the wider bandwidth of the noise compared to the limited bandwidth of the pulse-shaped signal.

% Noise Scaling logic
noise_scaling_factor = sqrt(P_y/P_n ./ 10.^(SNRdB./10) * (r/(1+alpha)));

Task 2 Observations

  • Multipath: Fading notches appear in the spectrum where destructive interference occurs between different propagation paths.
  • SNR: At low SNR (e.g., 0-10 dB), the noise floor rises, potentially masking the signal spectrum and closing the "eye" of the constellation.

4. Receiver Processing & Constellation Analysis

The receiver filters the incoming signal to maximize SNR (matched filtering) and samples the output at the symbol rate to recover the transmitted constellation.

Constellation Visualization

By comparing the 16-QAM received samples against the ideal grid, we can visually assess the system performance.

Task 3 Findings

Under b1 (Ideal) at 35dB, the constellation is sharp. As SNR decreases to 10dB, the clusters expand and begin to overlap, leading to Bit Error Rate (BER) degradation. With multipath (b2/b3), the constellation rotates and smears, demonstrating the need for channel equalization in a real-world receiver.

5. Receiver Phase Noise Impact

Non-ideal oscillators introduce phase jitter, modeled here using a free-running oscillator bandwidth ($\beta$).

% Applying Phase Noise
Beta = 100; % Bandwidth in Hz
pt = phasenoise(numel(xt), Fs, Beta);
rt = xt .* pt;

Task 4 Analysis

Phase noise causes a circular rotation of the constellation points. While low $\beta$ (100 Hz) shows minimal impact, a high $\beta$ (5000 Hz) results in significant angular spreading, making symbol detection nearly impossible without carrier phase recovery.

Conclusion

This experimentation highlights the trade-offs between modulation order, bandwidth, and robustness. While higher QAM orders increase data rates, they require significantly higher SNR and better oscillator stability to maintain reliable communication.
